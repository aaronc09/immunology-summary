ORIGIN OF ALL IMMUNE CELLS (HEMATOPOIESIS OVERVIEW)
All immune cells arise from:
Hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs)
   → Two main branches:

1. Lymphoid lineage (CLP)
   - B cells → plasma cells
   - T cells (helper CD4+, killer CD8+) (mature in thymus)
   - NK cells
   - Innate lymphoid cells (ILCs)- primarily reside in tissues-intestinal mucosa,lung, skin
   - plasmacytoid Dendritic Cells (pDCs)- circulates in blood

2. Myeloid lineage (CMP)
   2a. Granulocyte lineage
      • Neutrophils
      • Eosinophils
      • Basophils
   2b. Monocyte lineage
      • Monocytes (blood, short-lived)
         → Macrophages (tissue, long-lived)
         → monocyte-derived Dendritic Cells (moDCs) *formed during inflammation
   2c. Dendritic cell lineage (“true” dendritic cells)
      • Monocyte–Dendritic Progenitor (MDP)
         → Common Dendritic Progenitor (CDP)
            → conventional Dendritic Cells (cDC1, cDC2)
   2d. Mast cells
      • derived from myeloid progenitors
      • circulate as precursors → mature in tissues

WHITE BLOOD CELLS CIRCULATING IN THE BLOOD
1. Neutrophils (38–80%)
   - First responders
   - Phagocytosis, bacterial defense
2. Lymphocytes (15–49%)
   - B cells → antibody production
   - T cells → cell-mediated immunity
   - NK cells → kill infected/tumor cells
3. Monocytes (0–13%)
   - Circulate in blood
   - Differentiate into macrophages or moDCs in tissues
4. Eosinophils (0–8%)
   - Fight parasites
   - Involved in allergies and asthma
5. Basophils (0–2%)
   - Release histamine
   - Involved in allergic reactions

CYTOKINES
- Small signaling proteins secreted by immune cells (innate and adaptive)
- Coordinate and link innate and adaptive immunity

A. INTERLEUKINS (IL) – “What to do”
-Communication between white blood cells

B. INTERFERONS (IFN) – “Antiviral”
- Type I (IFN-α, IFN-β) (virus-infected cells + many others, esp. pDCs)
      → induce antiviral state
      → increase MHC class I expression
      → activate NK cells
- Type II (IFN-γ) (Th1 cells, NK cells)
      → activates macrophages
      → promotes cell-mediated immunity (Th1)
- Type III (IFN-λ) (epithelial cells)
      → mucosal antiviral defense (epithelial barriers)

C. TUMOR NECROSIS FACTOR (TNF) – “Inflammation / Cell death”
- TNF-α (macrophages, NK cells, T cells)
      → major pro-inflammatory cytokine
      → causes fever, inflammation
      → can lead to septic shock

D. CHEMOKINES – “Where to go”
-Subset of cytokines that direct immune cell migration (chemotaxis)
- CXCL8 (IL-8) (macrophages) 
         → attracts neutrophils
- CCL2 (macrophages/monocytes) 
         → attracts monocytes
- CXCL13 (follicular DCs, stromal cells inside B-cell follicles)  
*stromal cells are supporting cells of a tissue
*parenchymal cells do the main job (ex. Hepatocytes in liver, neurons in brain)
         →after activation Tfh cells upregulated CSCR5 receptors > migrate towards B-cell follicle

E. GROWTH FACTORS – “Grow / differentiate / repair (healing)”
-granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) 
         → neutrophil production
-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (M-CSF) 
         → macrophage development
-granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) 
         → granulocytes + macrophages
-“transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β)” (epithelial cells, stromal/fibroblast cells, dendritic cells, macrophages, Tregs, helper T cells, platelets)
         → promotes tissue repair and fibrosis by stimulating fibroblasts, extracellular matrix deposition, collagen production
         → promotes regulatory T cell (Treg) development
         → promote Th17 differentiation with IL-6/IL-23
-“vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)”
         → promotes blood vessel formation (angiogenesis)
         → essential for wound healing

F. ANTI-INFLAMMATORY / REGULATORY CYTOKINES – “Turn off / balance”
-Maintain immune homeostasis and prevent excessive inflammation
- IL-10 (macrophages, dendritic cells, Tregs)
      → suppresses cytokine production
      → inhibits macrophages and dendritic cells
      → reduces antigen presentation
- “transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β)” (see prevsiou section for which cells produce)
      → promotes Treg development
      → suppresses T cell activation, suppresses excessive immune activation, maintain immune tolerance, prevent autoimmunity
      → promotes class switching to IgA for mucosal immunity, gut/lung protection

IMMUNE RECEPTORS

A. (INNATE IMMUNITY) PATTERN RECOGNITION RECEPTORS (PRRs)   
-Found mainly in innate immune cells (dendritic cells, macrophages, neutrophils, also in epithelial barrier cells (skin, airway, gut, urinary tract); some in adaptive immune cells (but mainly act as co-stimulatory enhancers)
-PRRs recognize:
     (1) pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) 
         → conserved microbial structures
     (2) damage-associated molecular patterns(DAMPs) 
         → signals of cellular stress or injury
         >>Triggers cytokine secretion, upregulation of CD80/CD86 (on dendritic cells, macrophages, B cells), and activation of adaptive immunity

   1. Toll-Like Receptors (TLRs)
      - 10 types (TLR1–TLR10)
      - Located on cell surface and endosomal membranes
      - Rapid activation of inflammation and cytokine production

      (Cell surface TLRs)
      - Detect bacterial/fungal components
      - High: macrophages, cDCs, neutrophils, epithelial cells
      - Lower: B cells, some T cells, NK cells
      - Examples:
        - TLR1/2 → bacterial lipopeptides
        - TLR2/6 → gram-positive components
        - TLR4 → LPS (gram-negative bacteria)
        - TLR5 → flagellin
      (Endosomal TLRs)
      - Detect nucleic acids from pathogens
      - High: macrophages, cDCs, pDCs, B cells
      - Also: neutrophils, monocytes, epithelial cells
      - Examples:
        - TLR3 → dsRNA
        - TLR7/8 → ssRNA
        - TLR9 → CpG DNA

   2. C-Type Lectin Receptors (CLRs)
      -(High levels) macrophages, cDCs
      -(Moderate levels) neutrophils, epithelial cells
      -Surface receptors for carbohydrate-rich microbes
         >detect fungi and mycobacteria
         >trigger phagocytosis, cytokines, adaptive coordination

      - ~20 types
      - Examples:
        - Dectin-1 → beta-glucans
        - Dectin-2 → fungal/bacterial carbohydrates
        - Mannose receptor (CD206) → sugar binding, endocytosis
        - Mincle → fungi, mycobacteria, DAMPs
        - DC-SIGN → viral/bacterial/fungal binding
        - Langerin → skin surveillance

   3. NOD-Like Receptors (NLRs)
      -(high levels) macrophages, cDCs, neutrophils, monocytes
      -(also in) epithelial cells
      -Cytoplasmic sensors of bacterial products and stress
      -~20–25 types
      (Inflammasome-forming NLRs)
      -Detect microbes, ATP, crystals, ROS, mitochondrial damage
      -NLR → ASC → pro-caspase-1 → caspase-1 activation
      -Produces IL-1β, IL-18 + pyroptosis
      -Outcome: strong inflammation and neutrophil recruitment
      -ex. NLRP3, NLRP1, NLRC4, NLRP6, NLRP12
         *NLRC4 can function without ASC (ASC amplifies)
         *NLRP6/NLRP12 = context-dependent, often regulatory
      (Regulatory NLRs)
      -Limit excessive inflammation
      -ex. NLRX1, NLRP6, NLRP12
      (NOD sensors)
      -Detect peptidoglycan fragments
          >activate NF-κB, MAPK → TNF-α, IL-6, IL-8
      -ex. NOD1, NOD2

   4. RIG-I-Like Receptors (RLRs)
      -Expressed in many nucleated cells
      -Cytoplasmic viral RNA sensors
      -Types: RIG-I, MDA5, LGP2
          >Induce type I interferons (IFN-α, IFN-β)

   5. Cytosolic DNA Sensors
      (AIM2-like receptors / ALRs)
      -Detect dsDNA → ASC → caspase-1
          >produce IL-1β, IL-18 + pyroptosis
      (Regulatory DNA sensors)
      -Detect DNA in nucleus/cytoplasm
          >rRegulate interferon responses
      -ex. IFI16, MNDA, IFIX
      (cGAS-STING pathway)
      -cGAS detects dsDNA → produces cGAMP
      -cGAMP activates STING (ER membrane)
          >leads to type I interferon production

   6. Scavenger Receptors
      -(High levels) macrophages
      -(also) dendritic cells, neutrophils, endothelial, epithelial cells
      -Bind and clear altered/self/damaged molecules
          >Role: phagocytosis > signaling
      -ex. SR-A, MARCO, LOX-1, CD36

B. (ADAPTIVE IMMUNITY) SPECIFIC ANGTIGEN RECOGNITION RECEPTORS 
   - TCR 
   - BCR 
  + CO-RECEPTORS


C. CYTOKINE RECEPTORS – “Instruction / differentiation”

D. INHIBITORY CHECKPOINT RECEPTORS – “Turn OFF (Brake)”
   (Cells: mainly T cells; also NK cells, some B cells)
   - CTLA-4 
         → inhibits early activation (competes with CD28 for B7)
   - PD-1 
         → binds PD-L1/PD-L2 → suppresses T cells in tissues
   - LAG-3, TIM-3, TIGIT 
         → associated with T cell exhaustion

E. CHEMOKINE RECEPTORS – “Where to go (migration)”
   (Cells: all immune cells, cell-specific patterns)
   - CXCR1/2 
         → neutrophil migration
   - CXCR5 
         → B cells and Tfh to lymph node follicles

PHYSICAL BARRIERS

NEUTROPHILS
- Most abundant immune cells in the blood
- Lifespan: hours to days (very short-lived)
- Rapid-response “first responders” → arrive early at infection or injury
- After killing microbes → undergo apoptosis → cleared by macrophages (contributes to pus)

KILLING METHODS:
1. Phagocytosis & Intracellular Killing (primary mechanism)
   (Phagocytosis)
   - Engulf bacteria, fungi, some viruses
   - Recognize IgG-coated (Fc receptors) and C3b-coated microbes
   (Intracellular killing)
   - Respiratory (oxidative) burst via NADPH oxidase → ROS production
   - Phagosome fuses with lysosome → enzymatic killing

   *key antimicrobial components in enzymatic killing:
   - Proteases → degrade microbial proteins
   - Defensins → disrupt membranes
   - Lysozyme → breaks peptidoglycan (especially Gram+ bacteria)
   - Lactoferrin → binds iron, inhibits microbial growth

2. Degranulation
   - Release enzymes externally when targets are too large
   - Can cause host tissue damage and inflammation

3. Neutrophil Extracellular Traps (NETs)
   - DNA + histones + antimicrobial proteins form “nets”
   - Trap and kill microbes
   - NETosis: often leads to cell death (can also occur without death)
   - Excess NETs → tissue damage, thrombosis, autoimmunity

4. Cytokine & Chemokine Secretion

-----------------------------------------------------------------------

MONOCYTES
- Lifespan: 1–3 days in blood (up to ~5 days)

MAIN FUNCTIONS
a. Pathogen sensing (PRRs) + immune activation
   - Cytokines: TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-12
   - Chemokine production

b. Reserve cells → enter tissues and differentiate into:
   - Macrophages
   - monocyte-derived Dendritic Cells (moDCs)

OTHER FUNCTIONS
a. Phagocytosis (microbes, debris, dead cells)
b. Antigen presentation (MHC II; less potent than cDCs)
c. Chronic inflammation
   - Sustained recruitment → inflammatory macrophages
   - Seen in autoimmunity, atherosclerosis, fibrosis
d. Tissue repair & remodeling
   - Growth factor release
   - Angiogenesis support
   - Debris clearance

-----------------------------------------------------------------------

MACROPHAGES
- Derived from monocytes
- They are sentinel cells
- Lifespan: months to years (long-lived)
- Present in most tissues

TISSUE-SPECIFIC TYPES
- Brain → microglia
- Liver → Kupffer cells
- Lung → alveolar macrophages

FUNCTIONS
a. Phagocytosis (“garbage collectors”)
b. Pathogen sensing + early warning
   - Cytokines: TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6
c. Antigen presentation (MHC II)
   - Restimulate activated CD4+ T cells
d. Immune regulation
   - M1-like → pro-inflammatory
   - M2-like → anti-inflammatory, healing
e. Tissue repair & wound healing

conventional DENDRITIC CELLS (cDCs)-  “professional antigen presenting cells”
-star-shaped cells
-they are sentinel cells
-Lifespan: few days to ~2 weeks
-Not present in all tissues at all times

-Constantly sample environment (“wine drinkers”)
   >endocytosis → endosome → lysosome processing

ACTIVATION
-Triggered by PRRs + cytokines
   >produces cytokines and chemokines

   • CD8+ cytotoxic T cells   via MHC I (cross-presentation)

- Provide a “snapshot” of antigen exposure

ADDITIONAL NOTE

**INNATE IMMUNE RESPONSE TO VIRAL INFECTION**

plasmacytoid DENDRITIC CELLS (pDCs)
-derived primarily from lymphoid lineage (can also arise from myeloid progenitors)
-circulate in low numbers in blood and secondary lymphoid organs
   → recruited into tissues during infection
- Resting morphology: lymphocyte-like
- Activated morphology: dendritic (“starfish” shape)
- Lifespan: short-lived

VIRAL DETECTION:
-Virus enters via endocytosis or receptor-mediated uptake
   (C-type lectins, Fc receptors)
-Endosomal PRRs:
   • TLR7 → detects viral ssRNA
   • TLR9 → detects viral DNA

-Major producers of Type I interferons (IFN-α, IFN-β)
   → strongest source in early antiviral defense

-Antigen presentation
   - MHC II → CD4+ T cells
   - Can present via MHC I under certain conditions
   *Less potent than conventional dendritic cells (cDCs)
-Immune orchestration
   -Shape adaptive responses via cytokines
   -Influence:
      -T helper differentiation
      -CD8+ T cell activation/survival
       -B cell antibody production
-Immune regulation / tolerance
   -promote Treg development
   -produce immunoregulatory molecules
      → prevent excessive inflammation

*overactivation of pDCs by self-nucleic acids
   → chronic IFN production
   → implicated in:
      • SLE
      • Psoriasis

NATURAL KILLER (NK) CELLS
-Rapid responders (within hours)
   >provide early defense while adaptive immunity develops

TARGETS:
-Virus-infected cells
-Tumor cells
-Stressed cells
-Can also respond to some bacteria and parasites

MECHANISM OF RECOGNITION:
-MHC I 
   -NK cells have receptors (ex. KIR, NKG2A) that reads MHC I on cell surfaces
      >normal MHC I → no killing
      >low/absent MHC I → killing (“missing self”)
-stress ligands (ex. MICA, MICB, ULBPs) > surface proteins
      >NK has receptors (ex. NKG2D) that detects stress ligands 
-Antibody-Dependent Cellular Cytotoxicity (ADCC)
      >Fc receptors (CD16) on NK cells bind antibodies on target cells

-Growth and activation
   -IL-2 (from activated T cells) → stimulates NK proliferation
   -IL-15 is especially important for NK survival and development
-Crosstalk with macrophages
   ex. LPS stimulates macrophages → IL-12 production,  IL-12 stimulates NK cells → IFN-γ production, IFN-γ further activates macrophages  → positive feedback loop

ADHESION
-LFA-1 (Integrin α-L β-2, CD11a/CD18) on NK cells (also T cells, other leukocytes)
   >locks onto ICAM-1 (CD54) which is on most cells (upregulated on infected cells, tumor cells, cells exposed to inflammatory cytokines (ex. IFN-ℽ ,TNF)
   >>if both gets upregulated >> better adhesions

KILLING METHODS:
1. Perforin + granzymes
       >Perforin → forms pores
       >Granzymes → induce apoptosis
2. Fas–FasL (CD95) pathway
   - Fas ligand (on NK) binds Fas receptor (on target cells)
   → triggers apoptosis
*cytotoxic T cells also carries out step 1 and step 2 mechanism of killing  

CYTOKINE PRODUCTION
   - IFN-γ 
   - TNF-α
   → activate macrophages
   → enhance antigen presentation
   → promote Th1 responses
—----------------------------------------------------------------------------

THE COMPLEMENT SYSTEM
-About 20–30 plasma proteins (mainly produced by the liver) that circulate in inactive form
-Work together to destroy pathogens and amplify immune responses
-C3 is the central and most abundant component

ACTIVATION PATHWAYS (all converge at C3):
1. Classical pathway (antibody-dependent)
   - Triggered by antibodies (IgG or IgM) bound to antigen
   - Links adaptive immunity to complement activation

2. Alternative pathway (antibody-independent)
   - Triggered directly by microbial surfaces
   - Provides continuous low-level “surveillance” activation

3. Lectin pathway
   - Triggered by mannose-binding lectin (MBL)
   - MBL binds mannose on microbial surfaces (not found on healthy human cells)

COMMON PATHWAY:
- All pathways activate C3 → split into:
   • C3a
   • C3b

MAJOR FUNCTIONS:
1. Opsonization (“tagging for phagocytosis”)
   - C3b coats microbes
   - Enhances recognition by phagocytes (macrophages, neutrophils)

2. Inflammation & chemotaxis
   - C3a, C5a = anaphylatoxins
   → recruit immune cells (chemoattractants)
   → increase vascular permeability
   → promote inflammation

3. Direct killing (Membrane Attack Complex, MAC)
   - C5b–C9 form MAC
   → creates pores in microbial membranes
   → leads to cell lysis (especially Gram-negative bacteria)

CLINICAL NOTES (high-yield):
- Deficiency in complement → ↑ susceptibility to infections
- C5–C9 deficiency → Neisseria infections
- Overactivation → contributes to inflammation and autoimmune disease

T-cell activation (and helper T cell differentiation)
1. Initial activation steps
(signal 1)
-TCR on naive CD4 helper T cell binds:
   peptide antigen + MHC II on APC
*TCR on naive CD8 killer T cells binds to peptide antigen + MHC class I
-Mainly activates:
   NFAT pathway (via calcium/calcineurin signaling)

(signal 2 (costimulation))

   B7 (CD80/CD86) on APC

The cytokines present (secreted by APCs in the area) during full activation determine which helper T-cell subtype develop during the full activation:

(major helper T-cell subsets)  
-Th1   (formed from IL-12 environment during activation)
   → main cytokine produced:   IFN-γ (interferon gamma)
-main functions: intracellular pathogen defense, viruses, tuberculosis, macrophage activation, cell-mediated immunity
-Th2   (formed from IL-4 environment during activation)
   → main cytokines produced:  IL-4, IL-5, IL-13
-main functions: parasite defense, allergy/asthma, eosinophil activation, IgE class switching
-Th17   (formed from TGF-β + IL-6 environment during activation)       
 *developing Th17 cells often begin producing IL-21:
      autocrine signaling → stimulates themselves
      paracrine signaling → stimulates nearby Th17 cells (amplifies and reinforces the Th17 program)
   → main cytokine produced:  IL-17
-main functions: extracellular bacteria/fungi defense, neutrophil recruitment, mucosal inflammation
 *IL-23 (usually from dendritic cells/macrophages) becomes very important later:
      -stabilizes mature Th17 cells, supports survival, increases inflammatory/pathogenic function, strongly associated with autoimmune inflammation
-T follicular helper cells (Tfh)   (formed from IL-21 environment during activation)
   *Tfh cells mainly reside inside germinal centers within follicles of lymph nodes and spleen
   → main cytokine produced: IL-21
-main functions:  B-cell help, germinal center reactions, class switching, affinity maturation, memory B-cell formation
   *they can remain highly active while still expressing inhibitory receptors such as:
      PD-1 > this helps regulate potentially dangerous antibody responses during intense B-cell selection
-regulatory T cells (Treg)   (formed from IL-2 and TGF-β environment during activation)
   → main cytokines produced: IL-10 + TGF-β
-main functions: immune suppression, peripheral tolerance, prevention of autoimmunity
*T follicular regulatory cells (Tfr) 
-Tfr cells are a specialized regulatory T-cell subset that suppress immune activity inside germinal centers of lymph nodes and spleen  →  regulate Tfh cells, regulate B cells, prevent excessive or autoreactive antibody responses
   -common markers: CTLA-4, PD-1, CD25, FoxP3
    *Tfr cells usually express lower PD-1 levels than highly active Tfh cells

(additional helper T-cell subsets- less central) 
-Th9  (formed from IL-4 + TGF-β environment)
   → main cytokines produced: IL-9
-functions:   parasite defense, allergy, mast cell activation
-Th22   (formed from IL-6 + TNF-α environment)
   → main cytokines produced: IL-22
-functions: epithelial barrier, defense tissue repair, antimicrobial peptide production

B cell activation
-Two main types:
1. (T-independent B cell activation)
-non-protein antigens (polysaccharides, lipids, repetitive structures)
2 ways:
-(TI-1) T-independent type-1   (innate receptors)   
      ex. TLR-mediated activation (e.g., LPS)
-(TI-2) T- independent type2       (BCR-driven activation) 
      ex. strong BCR cross-linking (repetitive epitopes)
   *Complement-assisted activation (via CR2 coreceptor) is esp important for TI-2 responses (not as important for TI-1 since its signaling is already strong)
      
outcomes:
   →Mainly IgM production
   →Minimal class switching
   →No affinity maturation
   →Weak or no memory
   →Short-lived plasma cells

2 (T-dependent B-cell activation)
-Mainly driven by T follicular helper (Tfh) cells (inside follicles of secondary lymphoid organs)

Signal 1  
-BCR binds DIRECTLY to intact/native antigen (not via MHC)
-the B cell then internalizes the antigen, processes it into peptides, presents them on MHC class II 
-activated helper/Tfh cell with matching TCR specificity recognizes: peptide–MHC II complex on the B cell 

Signal 2 (essential costimulation)  
-activated Tfh/helper T cell expresses CD40L > binds to CD40 receptors on B cell
   >this is essential for full B-cell activation -germinal center formation, class switching, affinity maturation, memory formation 

Signal 3 (cytokine help) 
-key cytokines from the activating helper/Tfh cells help determine:
   -class switching 
   -proliferation 
   -plasma-cell differentiation 
   -germinal center behavior 
   -antibody response type
-IL-21 (core Tfh cytokine) -major germinal center cytokine -promotes B-cell proliferation -supports germinal center formation -drives plasma-cell differentiation -enhances affinity maturation and class switching
-IL-4 (commonly from Th2-like Tfh cells / Th2 cells) -activates STAT6 -promotes IgE (and some IgG subclass) switching -important for parasite/allergy responses
-IFN-γ (commonly from Th1-like Tfh cells / Th1 cells) -activates STAT1 -promotes certain opsonizing/complement-fixing IgG subclasses -important for intracellular pathogen responses
-TGF-β (from Tregs, helper T cells, dendritic cells, epithelial/stromal cells) -promotes IgA switching -important for mucosal immunity -contributes to immune regulation/tolerance
-IL-5 (often associated with Th2 responses) -supports IgA production -supports eosinophil responses
-IL-6 (mainly from APCs/innate immune cells) -supports plasma-cell differentiation -contributes to Tfh development and antibody responses

IMPORTANT CONCEPT -Signals 1–3 do NOT instantly determine the final antibody class -Instead, they activate and program the B-cell response -The eventual antibody output depends on: -germinal center dynamics -class-switch recombination -affinity maturation -cytokine environment -time/proliferation

POST-ACTIVATION FATES -After Signals 1–3, activated B cells can follow two major pathways

EXTRAFOLLICULAR RESPONSE (early rapid response) -occurs outside follicles -rapid plasma-cell generation -mainly produces IgM -low-affinity antibodies -short-lived plasma cells -limited or minimal memory formation -provides early temporary antibody protection
IMPORTANT: -IgM is the default initial antibody because naive B cells initially express IgM and IgD -Class switching requires: -CD40 signaling -cytokines -AID activation -time/proliferation -Therefore early extrafollicular responses are commonly IgM-dominant

GERMINAL CENTER (GC) REACTION (main high-quality adaptive response) -Activated B cells migrate into follicles of: -lymph nodes -spleen -Germinal centers form within follicles
DARK ZONE (proliferation + mutation phase) -B cells become centroblasts -Rapid proliferation occurs -Somatic hypermutation (SHM) of Ig variable regions -Class switch recombination (CSR) occurs -Key enzyme: AID (activation-induced cytidine deaminase) -AID is required for: -SHM -CSR -Purpose: -generate diversified antibody variants
LIGHT ZONE (selection + survival/instruction phase) -B cells become centrocytes -B cells compete for intact antigen displayed on: -follicular dendritic cells (FDCs) -Only higher-affinity B cells capture enough antigen -B cells then present peptide–MHC II to Tfh cells -Tfh cells provide: -CD40L -IL-21 and other cytokines -survival signals -Lower-affinity B cells fail selection and undergo apoptosis

CLASS SWITCHING INFLUENCES -Tfh cytokines help determine antibody isotype: -IL-4 → IgE (and some IgG subclasses) -IFN-γ → certain IgG subclasses -TGF-β → IgA -IL-21 → enhances proliferation, plasma-cell differentiation, and switching

CYCLIC AFFINITY MATURATION LOOP -B cells repeatedly cycle between zones: -Dark zone: -proliferation -mutation -Light zone: -selection -Tfh interaction -survival testing -Repeated cycles progressively increase antibody affinity

Outcomes:
   →Class-switched antibodies (IgG, IgA, IgE) 
   →Affinity maturation, resulting in high-affinity antibodies 
   →Memory B cells (long-term immunity) 
   →Long-lived plasma cells (sustained antibody production)

ANTIBODY TYPES:
IgM
- First produced
- Pentamer
- Strong complement activator
- Short half-life (~5 days)

IgG
- Most abundant in blood
- Opsonization (phagocytosis)
- “antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC)” via NK cells
- Long half-life (~21 days)
- Crosses placenta


IgA
- Mucosal immunity (GI, respiratory, saliva, tears, breast milk)
- Dimer
- Resistant to degradation

IgE
- Parasites (helminths)
- Binds basophils and mast cells 
- Allergy and anaphylaxis

IgD
- B cell receptor on naïve B cells (with IgM)
- Regulates activation
- Minimal circulating levels
